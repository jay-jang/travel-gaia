> An Infrastructure Manager (IM) is any body or firm responsible for establishing, managing, and maintaining railway infrastructure — including track, signalling, electrification, stations, and traffic management — as defined in EU Directive 2012/34/EU establishing a Single European Railway Area. The IM grants train operators access to the network, sets track access charges, and allocates train paths (capacity), operating separately from the Train Operating Companies (TOCs) that run passenger services on the infrastructure.

EU railway liberalisation — begun with Directive 91/440/EEC and consolidated into the current framework by Directive 2012/34/EU (the Recast Directive) and its subsequent amendments — requires member states to separate the functions of infrastructure management from train operation to prevent anti-competitive conflicts of interest. The IM must provide non-discriminatory access to the network under a published Network Statement that details the infrastructure, access conditions, pricing principles, and capacity allocation process. IMs include major entities such as Network Rail (Great Britain), SNCF Réseau (France), DB InfraGO (Germany), RFI (Italy), Adif (Spain), and Infrabel (Belgium). The track access charge levied by the IM on train operators must be cost-based and non-discriminatory. Capacity is allocated through a timetabling process in which the IM coordinates competing requests and produces a Working Timetable; conflicts are resolved through coordination procedures. The ERA (European Union Agency for Railways) oversees technical interoperability and safety certification of rolling stock and infrastructure across the network, while national regulatory bodies (NRBs) oversee the economic regulation of IMs. The OSDM rail distribution standard and the IRT/NRT ticketing architecture reference the IM as the entity that controls the physical path on which ticket validity is conditioned.

# Distinctions
- **Infrastructure Manager** vs [Train Operating Company](/ground/rail/train-operating-company.md) — A Train Operating Company (TOC) holds a licence or franchise to run passenger or freight services on the railway network; the Infrastructure Manager (IM) owns, develops, and maintains the physical railway network on which the TOC operates. EU law requires these functions to be legally separated or at minimum to have management independence, so the IM cannot favour its own train operations over competitors.
- **Infrastructure Manager** vs [Track Access Charge](/ground/rail/track-access-charge.md) — The Track Access Charge is the fee that the Infrastructure Manager charges train operators for the right to run on a specific section of the network; the Infrastructure Manager is the entity that sets and collects this charge under the framework established by the national regulatory body.
