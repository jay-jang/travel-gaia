> A Track Access Charge (TAC) is the fee paid by a licensed train operating company to the infrastructure manager for the right to use the national rail network — its tracks, stations, signalling, and electrification — to run passenger or freight services. It is the central pricing mechanism of the vertically separated, liberalised rail market mandated by the EU Single European Railway Area framework.

Track access charges are the financial instrument through which infrastructure managers recover the costs of building, maintaining, and renewing the rail network, supplemented by state funding where direct recovery alone would be insufficient. EU Directive 2012/34/EU (the "Single European Railway Area Directive") requires member states to separate infrastructure management from train operations and to establish access charges based on the minimum cost of operating a train service, potentially supplemented by mark-ups for scarcity (congested paths) and full cost recovery contributions. In Great Britain, Network Rail / Great British Railways sets a charging framework approved by the Office of Rail and Road (ORR). Train Operating Companies budget TAC as their single largest cost item after staff.

**한국어 / Korean** — **선로 사용료(Track Access Charge)** — 선로 사용료(TAC)는 면허 있는 철도 운영사가 여객 또는 화물 서비스 운행을 위해 국가 철도 네트워크를 사용하는 권리에 대해 인프라 관리자에게 지급하는 요금이다. EU 단일 유럽 철도 구역 체계가 의무화한 수직 분리·자유화 철도 시장의 핵심 가격 메커니즘이다.

# Distinctions
- **Track Access Charge** vs [Train Operating Company](/ground/rail/train-operating-company.md) — A Train Operating Company is the entity that pays track access charges to the infrastructure manager in exchange for path rights; the Track Access Charge is the financial instrument — the pricing mechanism — through which the infrastructure manager monetises network access.
- **Track Access Charge** vs [Rail Interoperability Directive (EU 2016/797)](/ground/rail/rail-interoperability-directive.md) — The Rail Interoperability Directive establishes technical standards ensuring rolling stock and systems can operate seamlessly across borders; the Track Access Charge Directive (2012/34/EU) is the separate legislation governing the commercial and financial conditions of rail network access, including charge-setting principles.
