> MARPOL (International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, 1973/1978) is the IMO treaty that sets binding global standards to prevent marine and atmospheric pollution caused by ship operations. Its six annexes cover oil discharges (I), noxious liquid substances (II), packaged harmful goods (III), sewage (IV), garbage (V), and air emissions (VI). Annex VI is most consequential for the cruise industry: it establishes global sulphur-oxide and nitrogen-oxide emission limits and provides the framework for the IMO GHG reduction strategy, including the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII).

MARPOL was originally adopted in 1973 and substantially amended by the 1978 Protocol (hence "73/78"). It entered into force in 1983 and is in force for over 160 flag states covering more than 99% of the world's merchant shipping tonnage. Annex VI (Regulations for the Prevention of Air Pollution from Ships), adopted in 1997 and in force since 2005, is the most rapidly evolving annex for the cruise sector. Key requirements include: (1) the global 0.50% m/m sulphur cap on fuel oil (in force 1 January 2020) and the stricter 0.10% cap in Emission Control Areas (ECAs) such as the Baltic Sea, North Sea, and North American ECA; (2) NOx Tier III limits in NOx ECAs for new ships; (3) the Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI) for new ships (Regulation 20) and the Ship Energy Efficiency Management Plan (SEEMP) for all ships (Regulation 22); and (4) the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) and annual rating system (in force 1 January 2023). MARPOL amendments are adopted by the MEPC (Marine Environment Protection Committee) of IMO.

# Distinctions
- **MARPOL** vs [SOLAS](/cruise/cruise/solas.md) — SOLAS (Safety of Life at Sea) governs the physical safety of ships and persons on board — hull construction, fire protection, life-saving appliances, and ship security; MARPOL governs the environmental impact of ship operations, covering oil, chemical, sewage, garbage, and air-emission discharges.
- **MARPOL** vs [Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII)](/common/sustainability/carbon-intensity-indicator-cii.md) — CII is the annual operational efficiency rating metric for individual ships, defined under MARPOL Annex VI Regulation 28 and measured in grams of CO2 per cargo-carrying capacity and nautical mile; MARPOL is the overarching international convention under which CII was established and under which its rules are enforced by flag states.
