> The act of refusing carriage to a passenger holding a confirmed reservation against their will when a flight is oversold and not enough volunteers come forward. Under US DOT 14 CFR Part 250 it is the trigger event for mandatory denied boarding compensation, and it is the involuntary counterpart to voluntary denied boarding.

Part 250 requires carriers to first solicit volunteers (VDB); only if too few volunteer may the airline involuntarily deny boarding, applying a non-discriminatory boarding-priority rule it has established. A passenger involuntarily denied boarding is owed fixed compensation under 14 CFR 250.5 (currently up to 200% of the one-way fare to USD 1,075, or up to 400% to USD 2,150 for longer delays, with no compensation if re-accommodated to arrive within one hour). IDB is distinct from a flight cancellation or delay, and from the broader commercial practice of overbooking that creates the oversale condition.

**한국어 / Korean** — **비자발적 탑승 거부 (Involuntary Denied Boarding, IDB)** — 항공편이 초과 판매되어 자원자가 충분하지 않을 때, 확약된 예약을 보유한 승객의 의사에 반하여 탑승을 거부하는 행위다. 미국 DOT 14 CFR Part 250에 따라 의무적 denied boarding compensation의 발동 사유이며, voluntary denied boarding에 대응하는 비자발적 절차다.

Part 250은 항공사가 먼저 자원자(VDB)를 모집하도록 요구하며, 자원자가 부족한 경우에만 사전에 수립한 비차별적 탑승 우선순위 규칙에 따라 비자발적으로 탑승을 거부할 수 있다. 비자발적으로 탑승 거부된 승객은 14 CFR 250.5에 따라 정액 보상을 받는다(현재 편도 운임의 200%까지 USD 1,075, 지연이 더 긴 경우 400%까지 USD 2,150, 1시간 이내 도착으로 재배정 시 보상 없음). IDB는 항공편 취소나 지연, 그리고 초과 판매 상태를 만드는 더 광범위한 상업적 관행인 overbooking과 구별된다.

# Distinctions
- **Involuntary Denied Boarding** vs [Voluntary Denied Boarding](/common/disruption/voluntary-denied-boarding.md) — IDB is forced bumping against the passenger's will with statutory compensation; VDB is a negotiated giving-up of a seat for an agreed amount, and airlines must seek volunteers before bumping anyone involuntarily.
- **Involuntary Denied Boarding** vs [Overbooking](/air/air-shop/overbooking.md) — Overbooking is the revenue-management practice of selling more seats than exist; IDB is the regulated event that may follow when overbooking results in an oversale at the gate.
